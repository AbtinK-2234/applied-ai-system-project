# Pet Nutrition Guide

## Dog Nutrition

### Daily Feeding Guidelines
Dogs should be fed based on their weight, age, and activity level. Puppies (under 1 year) need 3-4 meals per day, while adult dogs typically do well with 2 meals per day. Senior dogs (7+ years) may benefit from smaller, more frequent meals to aid digestion.

### Portion Sizes by Weight
- Small dogs (under 10 kg): 1/2 to 1 cup of dry food per day
- Medium dogs (10-25 kg): 1 to 2 cups per day
- Large dogs (25-45 kg): 2 to 3 cups per day
- Giant breeds (over 45 kg): 3 to 5 cups per day

These are general guidelines. Always consult your veterinarian for precise amounts based on your dog's specific needs and the caloric density of the food you use.

### Essential Nutrients for Dogs
Dogs require a balanced diet including protein (18-25% for adults), fats (10-15%), carbohydrates, vitamins (A, D, E, K, B-complex), and minerals (calcium, phosphorus, iron). High-quality commercial dog food typically provides complete nutrition.

### Foods Toxic to Dogs
Never feed dogs chocolate, grapes, raisins, onions, garlic, xylitol (artificial sweetener), macadamia nuts, alcohol, caffeine, or cooked bones (which can splinter). If your dog ingests any of these, contact your veterinarian immediately.

### Hydration
Dogs need approximately 30-50 ml of water per kilogram of body weight daily. Always provide fresh, clean water. Increased water intake is needed during hot weather, after exercise, or if the dog eats primarily dry food.

## Cat Nutrition

### Daily Feeding Guidelines
Cats are obligate carnivores and require a meat-based diet. Adult cats should be fed 2 meals per day. Kittens (under 1 year) need 3-4 meals per day. Free-feeding dry food can lead to obesity; measured meals are recommended.

### Portion Sizes
- Average adult cat (4-5 kg): 200-250 calories per day
- Kittens: require approximately twice the calories per kilogram as adults
- Senior cats (10+ years): may need 10-20% fewer calories

### Essential Nutrients for Cats
Cats require taurine (found only in animal tissue), arachidonic acid, vitamin A (they cannot convert beta-carotene), and high-quality animal protein. A deficiency in taurine can cause serious heart and eye problems.

### Foods Toxic to Cats
Keep cats away from onions, garlic, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, grapes, raisins, raw eggs, raw fish (in large amounts), and dog food (lacks essential cat nutrients like taurine). Lilies are extremely toxic to cats even in small amounts.

### Hydration
Cats often have a low thirst drive. Wet food helps with hydration (contains ~75% water). A water fountain can encourage drinking. Cats need approximately 50 ml of water per kilogram of body weight daily. Signs of dehydration include lethargy, dry gums, and skin tenting.

## General Nutrition Tips

### Transitioning Foods
When switching pet food, do so gradually over 7-10 days. Mix increasing amounts of new food with decreasing amounts of old food to prevent digestive upset.

### Treats
Treats should make up no more than 10% of a pet's daily caloric intake. Use small, low-calorie treats for training. Many fruits and vegetables make healthy treats for dogs (carrots, blueberries, apple slices without seeds).

### Weight Management
Obesity is a leading health concern in pets. You should be able to feel your pet's ribs without pressing hard. If you cannot, consider reducing portions or switching to a weight management formula. Regular weigh-ins help track progress.

### Scheduled vs. Free Feeding
Scheduled feeding (set meal times) is generally recommended over free feeding because it helps control portions, makes it easier to monitor appetite changes (which can indicate illness), and supports training routines.
