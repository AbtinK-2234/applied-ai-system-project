# Pet Health Guide

## Preventive Care

### Vaccination Schedule for Dogs
- 6-8 weeks: Distemper, Parvovirus
- 10-12 weeks: DHPP (Distemper, Hepatitis, Parainfluenza, Parvovirus)
- 14-16 weeks: DHPP booster, Rabies
- Annually: Rabies booster (or every 3 years depending on local laws), DHPP booster, Bordetella (kennel cough) if boarding or socializing frequently

### Vaccination Schedule for Cats
- 6-8 weeks: FVRCP (Feline Viral Rhinotracheitis, Calicivirus, Panleukopenia)
- 10-12 weeks: FVRCP booster
- 14-16 weeks: FVRCP booster, Rabies
- Annually: Rabies booster, FVRCP booster
- FeLV (Feline Leukemia) vaccine recommended for outdoor cats

### Parasite Prevention
Year-round prevention is recommended for fleas, ticks, and heartworm. Monthly topical or oral preventatives are available. Heartworm is transmitted by mosquitoes and is potentially fatal if untreated. Regular fecal exams (1-2 times per year) check for intestinal parasites.

### Dental Health
Dental disease affects over 80% of pets by age 3. Brush your pet's teeth daily or at least 3 times per week. Dental chews and water additives can supplement brushing. Professional dental cleanings under anesthesia are recommended annually. Signs of dental problems include bad breath, difficulty eating, drooling, and pawing at the mouth.

## Common Health Issues

### Dogs
- **Hip Dysplasia**: Common in large breeds. Symptoms include limping, difficulty rising, reluctance to climb stairs. Managed with weight control, exercise modification, anti-inflammatory medication, and sometimes surgery.
- **Ear Infections**: Frequent in floppy-eared breeds. Symptoms include head shaking, scratching, odor, and discharge. Clean ears regularly and keep them dry after swimming.
- **Allergies**: Can be environmental, food-related, or flea-related. Symptoms include itching, licking, red skin, and ear infections. Elimination diets can help identify food allergens.
- **Gastrointestinal Issues**: Vomiting and diarrhea can result from dietary indiscretion, infections, or stress. Withhold food for 12-24 hours, then offer bland diet (boiled chicken and rice). See a vet if symptoms persist beyond 24-48 hours.

### Cats
- **Urinary Tract Issues**: Common in male cats. Symptoms include frequent urination, straining, crying while urinating, blood in urine. A blocked male cat is a medical emergency requiring immediate veterinary care.
- **Upper Respiratory Infections**: Sneezing, nasal discharge, eye discharge, lethargy. Usually viral (herpesvirus, calicivirus). Keep the cat comfortable, ensure eating and drinking. See a vet if symptoms worsen or persist.
- **Hyperthyroidism**: Common in older cats. Symptoms include weight loss despite increased appetite, hyperactivity, vomiting. Diagnosed with blood test. Treatable with medication, diet, or radioactive iodine therapy.
- **Kidney Disease**: Very common in senior cats. Symptoms include increased thirst and urination, weight loss, decreased appetite. Early detection through regular blood work is important. Managed with diet, fluids, and medication.

## When to See a Veterinarian Urgently

Seek immediate veterinary care if your pet shows any of these signs:
- Difficulty breathing or rapid breathing
- Inability to urinate (especially male cats)
- Bloated or distended abdomen (especially large-breed dogs)
- Seizures
- Collapse or inability to stand
- Profuse bleeding
- Ingestion of toxic substances
- Severe vomiting or diarrhea (more than 24 hours, or with blood)
- Pale or blue gums
- Eye injuries

## Regular Health Monitoring

### At-Home Health Checks
Perform weekly checks of your pet's eyes (should be clear and bright), ears (clean, no odor), teeth and gums (pink gums, no swelling), skin and coat (no lumps, bumps, or excessive shedding), and weight. Track any changes and report them to your veterinarian.

### Veterinary Visit Frequency
- Puppies and kittens: every 3-4 weeks until 16 weeks old
- Adult pets (1-7 years): annual wellness exam
- Senior pets (7+ years): twice-yearly wellness exams with blood work

### Pet First Aid Kit Essentials
Keep on hand: gauze, adhesive tape, hydrogen peroxide (to induce vomiting only on veterinary advice), digital thermometer, tweezers, antiseptic wipes, emergency vet phone number, and any prescribed medications.
