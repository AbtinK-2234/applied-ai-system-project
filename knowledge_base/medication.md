# Pet Medication and Supplement Guide

## Administering Medication

### Giving Pills to Dogs
- **Hide in food**: Wrap the pill in a small piece of cheese, peanut butter (xylitol-free), or a commercial pill pocket. Give a plain treat first, then the treat with the pill, then another plain treat.
- **Direct administration**: Open the mouth gently, place the pill as far back on the tongue as possible, close the mouth and gently stroke the throat until the dog swallows. Follow with water or a treat.
- **Crush and mix** (only if the medication allows it - check with your vet): Some pills can be crushed and mixed with a small amount of wet food. Time-release and enteric-coated pills should never be crushed.

### Giving Pills to Cats
- **Pill pockets**: Commercial pill pockets designed for cats work well for many cats
- **Pill popper/gun**: A plastic syringe-like device that places the pill at the back of the throat. Less stressful than using fingers.
- **Compounded medication**: Many medications can be compounded into flavored liquids or transdermal gels that are applied to the ear flap. Ask your veterinarian about compounding options for difficult cats.

### Liquid Medications
Use the provided syringe or dropper. Insert into the side of the mouth (between the cheek and teeth), not directly down the throat (risk of aspiration). Administer slowly, allowing the pet to swallow between squirts. Mix with a small amount of food if permitted.

### Topical Medications
Apply to clean, dry skin. Part the fur to reach the skin surface. For flea/tick preventatives, apply between the shoulder blades where the pet cannot lick. Prevent licking of topical treatments - use an e-collar if necessary. Wash hands after application.

## Common Medications

### Pain Management
- **NSAIDs for dogs**: Carprofen (Rimadyl), meloxicam (Metacam). Never give human NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) to pets - they are toxic.
- **NSAIDs for cats**: Meloxicam (very careful dosing - cats are extremely sensitive to NSAIDs). Only use under strict veterinary supervision.
- **Gabapentin**: Used for pain management in both dogs and cats. Also helps with anxiety. May cause sedation.
- **Tramadol**: Used for moderate pain in dogs. Less commonly used in cats.

### Antibiotics
Always complete the full course of antibiotics even if symptoms improve. Common antibiotics include amoxicillin, cephalexin, and metronidazole. Store as directed (some require refrigeration). Watch for side effects: vomiting, diarrhea, loss of appetite.

### Flea and Tick Prevention
- **Oral preventatives**: Simparica, NexGard, Bravecto (dogs); Comfortis (cats). Given monthly or every 3 months depending on product.
- **Topical preventatives**: Frontline, Advantage, Revolution. Applied monthly between shoulder blades.
- **Collars**: Seresto collar provides 8 months of protection.
- Year-round prevention is recommended in most climates.

### Heartworm Prevention
- Monthly preventatives: Heartgard (ivermectin), Interceptor (milbemycin), Sentinel, ProHeart injection (every 6-12 months)
- Annual heartworm testing recommended even with regular prevention
- Cats can also get heartworm; prevention is available (Revolution covers both flea and heartworm in cats)

## Supplements

### Joint Health
- **Glucosamine and chondroitin**: Support cartilage health. Beneficial for senior pets and breeds prone to joint issues. Takes 4-6 weeks to see effects.
- **Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil)**: Anti-inflammatory properties that support joints, skin, coat, heart, and brain health. Use pet-specific products for correct dosing.
- **MSM (Methylsulfonylmethane)**: May reduce inflammation and support joint comfort.

### Digestive Health
- **Probiotics**: Support healthy gut bacteria. Useful during antibiotic treatment, after dietary changes, or for pets with chronic digestive issues. Use pet-specific strains.
- **Pumpkin (plain, canned)**: Natural fiber source that helps with both diarrhea and constipation. 1-2 tablespoons for dogs, 1 teaspoon for cats.

### Skin and Coat
- **Fish oil**: Omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids improve coat shine and reduce itching
- **Vitamin E**: Supports skin health. Often included in quality pet foods.
- **Biotin**: May improve coat quality in pets with deficiencies.

## Medication Safety

### Storage
- Store medications in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight unless otherwise specified
- Keep all medications out of reach of pets and children
- Check expiration dates regularly and dispose of expired medications properly
- Never share medications between pets without veterinary approval

### Medication Interactions
Always inform your veterinarian about all medications, supplements, and treats your pet receives. Some common interactions:
- NSAIDs should not be combined with corticosteroids (risk of gastrointestinal ulceration)
- Some antibiotics interfere with flea/tick preventatives
- Certain supplements can affect medication absorption

### Scheduling Medications
- Give medications at the same time each day for consistent blood levels
- Some medications must be given with food; others on an empty stomach - follow label directions
- Set reminders or alarms to maintain consistent dosing schedules
- If you miss a dose, give it as soon as you remember unless it's close to the next dose - then skip the missed dose. Never double up.

### Signs of Adverse Reactions
Contact your veterinarian if you notice:
- Vomiting or diarrhea after starting a new medication
- Lethargy or behavioral changes
- Loss of appetite
- Skin rash or hives
- Difficulty breathing
- Swelling of the face or extremities
