# Pet Training and Behavior Guide

## Dog Training Fundamentals

### Positive Reinforcement
Positive reinforcement is the most effective and humane training method. Reward desired behaviors with treats, praise, or play immediately (within 1-2 seconds) after the behavior. Timing is critical - delayed rewards confuse dogs about which behavior is being reinforced.

### Basic Commands Every Dog Should Know
1. **Sit**: Hold a treat above the dog's nose, move it slowly back over the head. As the dog looks up and naturally sits, say "sit" and reward immediately.
2. **Stay**: Ask the dog to sit, hold your palm out and say "stay," take one step back. If the dog stays, reward. Gradually increase distance and duration.
3. **Come (recall)**: Start in a low-distraction environment. Say "come" in an excited voice while moving backward. Reward generously when the dog reaches you. Never punish a dog for coming to you, even if they were doing something wrong before.
4. **Down**: From a sit, lure the dog down with a treat moving from nose to floor. Reward when elbows touch the ground.
5. **Leave it**: Place a treat on the floor, cover with your hand. When the dog stops trying to get it, reward with a different, better treat. Teaches impulse control.

### Training Session Guidelines
- Keep sessions short: 5-10 minutes for puppies, 10-15 minutes for adults
- End on a positive note, even if it means asking for an easy command
- Train before meals when the dog is motivated by food
- Practice in different environments to generalize commands
- Be consistent with commands - use the same word and hand signal each time
- All family members should use the same commands and rules

### Common Behavior Problems in Dogs
- **Jumping on people**: Turn away and ignore the dog when it jumps. Reward four-on-the-floor behavior. Consistency from all people is essential.
- **Excessive barking**: Identify the trigger (boredom, alert, attention-seeking, anxiety). Address the root cause rather than just suppressing the barking.
- **Leash pulling**: Stop walking when the dog pulls. Resume only when the leash is loose. Consider a front-clip harness for management while training.
- **Separation anxiety**: Build independence gradually. Practice short departures. Provide engaging toys when leaving. Severe cases may need professional help and possibly medication.
- **Resource guarding**: Do not punish - this often escalates the behavior. Trade up (offer something better in exchange for the guarded item). Consult a professional trainer for serious guarding.

## Cat Training and Behavior

### Can Cats Be Trained?
Cats are highly trainable but motivated differently than dogs. They respond best to food rewards and short, frequent sessions. Clicker training works well - the click marks the exact moment of the desired behavior.

### Useful Cat Training
- **Come when called**: Associate your call with high-value treats. Start close, gradually increase distance.
- **Carrier training**: Leave the carrier out with comfortable bedding. Toss treats inside regularly. This reduces stress for vet visits.
- **Sit**: Hold a treat above the cat's head and move it back slightly. Most cats will sit to look up. Click and treat.
- **High-five/shake**: Shape the behavior by rewarding any paw movement, then specifically raised paw, then paw touching your hand.

### Common Cat Behavior Issues
- **Scratching furniture**: Provide appropriate scratching surfaces near the furniture they target. Use double-sided tape or citrus spray on furniture as deterrents. Never declaw - it's an amputation that causes lasting pain and behavioral problems.
- **Litter box avoidance**: Rule out medical issues first. Ensure one more litter box than number of cats. Scoop daily. Try different litter types. Place boxes in quiet, accessible locations.
- **Nighttime activity**: Increase play and feeding before bedtime. Ignore attention-seeking behavior at night (responding reinforces it). Provide interactive toys for solo play.
- **Aggression during play**: Never use hands as toys. Redirect to appropriate toys. End play if the cat becomes too rough. Provide multiple daily play sessions to burn energy.
- **Inter-cat conflict**: Ensure sufficient resources (food bowls, litter boxes, resting spots) for all cats. Vertical space reduces territorial tension. Reintroduce gradually if needed.

## Socialization

### Puppy Socialization (Critical Period: 3-14 weeks)
Expose puppies to a wide variety of people, animals, sounds, surfaces, and environments in a positive way. This is the single most important thing you can do for a puppy's long-term behavior. Carry puppies to new places if not fully vaccinated. Puppy classes provide controlled socialization.

### Adult Dog Socialization
Older dogs can still learn to be more comfortable in new situations, but progress is slower. Go at the dog's pace. Pair new experiences with treats. Never force a fearful dog into a situation.

### Cat Socialization
Kittens should be handled gently by different people during weeks 2-7. Expose to various sounds, surfaces, and experiences. Adult cats can become more social with patience and positive associations (treats, play).

## When to Seek Professional Help

Consider a certified professional dog trainer (CPDT-KA) or veterinary behaviorist if:
- Aggression toward people or animals
- Severe fear or anxiety that doesn't improve
- Compulsive behaviors (tail chasing, excessive licking)
- Behavior problems that are getting worse despite your efforts
- Any sudden behavior change (rule out medical causes first)
